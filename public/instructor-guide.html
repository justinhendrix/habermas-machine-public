Habermas Machine
Instructor Guide — Classroom Deliberation Simulator

This guide walks you through running an AI-mediated group deliberation exercise in a 90-minute Zoom or in-person class session. The exercise is inspired by the research protocol in "Can AI Mediation Improve Democratic Deliberation?" (Tessler et al., arXiv, 2026).

Important: This is a pedagogical simulation, not a validated civic decision system. The AI may make mistakes in summarizing opinions — that's partly the point of the exercise.

Before Class

Setup Checklist

  1. Go to your deployment URL + /instructor
  2. Enter your instructor secret
  3. Choose or write a deliberation question (see suggestions below)
  4. Set group size (recommended: 4–6 students)
  5. Click Create Session
  6. Note the 6-character session code — you'll share this with students
  7. Test the flow yourself: open the student URL in an incognito window, join with a test name

Suggested Deliberation Questions

  • Should AI systems be required to disclose when they are interacting with humans?
  • Should social media platforms be liable for algorithmic amplification of harmful content?
  • Should governments ban the use of facial recognition technology in public spaces?
  • Should there be a universal basic income to address AI-driven job displacement?
  • Should large language models be regulated as public utilities?
  • Should children under 16 be prohibited from using social media?
Tip: The best questions have genuine disagreement among reasonable people. Avoid questions where your class is likely to be unanimous — the exercise is most illuminating when the AI must navigate real differences.

The Protocol Explained

Share this with students at the start:

  1. Write — You write your individual opinion on a question.
  2. Generate — An AI reads all opinions in your group and drafts 4 candidate "group statements" that try to capture common ground.
  3. Rank — You rank the 4 candidates from best to worst. The group selects a winner using Borda count voting.
  4. Critique — You critique the winning statement: what's good, what's missing, what feels unfair.
  5. Revise — The AI revises the statements using your critiques.
  6. Rank again — You rank the revised candidates. A new winner is chosen.
  7. Compare — You decide: was the revised statement better than the initial one?

Ranking Method: Borda Count

Each student ranks all 4 candidates. Points are assigned:

  • 1st choice → 4 points
  • 2nd choice → 3 points
  • 3rd choice → 2 points
  • 4th choice → 1 point

The candidate with the most total points wins. Ties are broken by number of first-place votes. This is a simple, transparent method — other methods (instant runoff, Condorcet) could produce different results.

Debrief Discussion Questions

About the AI's Role

  1. Did the AI accurately capture your group's views? What did it get right or wrong?
  2. Were minority perspectives preserved, or were they flattened into consensus language?
  3. Did the AI invent any positions that nobody actually held?
  4. How did the 4 candidates differ from each other? Were the differences meaningful?

About the Process

  1. Did the critique-and-revision cycle actually improve the statement? What changed?
  2. How does ranking 4 options differ from writing a group statement yourself? What's gained or lost?
  3. Could a different ranking method (e.g., majority rule, consensus requirement) have changed the outcome?
  4. Did you feel represented in the winning statement? Why or why not?

About AI and Democracy

  1. Would you trust a process like this for real civic decisions (e.g., town halls, citizen assemblies)? What would need to change?
  2. Who benefits from AI-mediated deliberation? Who might be disadvantaged?
  3. Does AI mediation clarify disagreement or obscure it?
  4. What's the difference between "common ground" and "forced consensus"?

Pedagogical Themes

This exercise is designed to help students think critically about:

  • Common ground vs. forced consensus — Does synthesizing views help groups find agreement, or does it paper over real differences?
  • Fairness in aggregation — The choice of voting method shapes the outcome. Borda count, majority rule, and consensus each encode different values.
  • Minority viewpoint representation — AI synthesis can either amplify or silence minority perspectives. Watch for this.
  • Generation vs. selection — Students don't write the group statement; they choose among AI-generated options. What does this do to ownership and agency?
  • Critique-and-revision as deliberation — The paper's key insight: iterative feedback can improve AI-mediated outcomes. Does it?
  • Transparency and legibility — Can people meaningfully participate in AI-mediated processes if they don't understand how the AI works?

Connection to the Research

The original Habermas Machine protocol (Tessler et al., 2024) used:

  • A specially trained generative model fine-tuned to produce group statements
  • A personalized reward model trained on individual preferences
  • Bayesian aggregation over individual reward models to select winners
  • Large-scale experiments with thousands of participants in the UK

This classroom version uses:

  • Prompt-based generation with a general-purpose LLM (Claude Haiku)
  • Borda count voting instead of personalized reward models
  • Small groups (4–6 students) instead of population-scale samples
  • Manual phase advancement by the instructor

The pedagogical logic is preserved: individual input → AI synthesis → selection → critique → revision → comparison. But this is a simulation of the protocol, not a reproduction of the research.
